Muscular System ü¶æ

The Body's Engine: Structure, Function & Types

What is it?

A complex system of over 600 contractile tissues (muscles) that enables movement, maintains posture, and generates heat.

Three Types

Skeletal (voluntary, moves bones), Smooth (involuntary, lines organs), and Cardiac (involuntary, found only in the heart).

Key Fact

Muscles make up approximately 40% of your total body weight and work by contracting (shortening) and relaxing (lengthening).

Overview & Properties üìñ

Introduction to Myology

  • üî¨Definition

    The muscular system is composed of specialized cells called muscle fibers. Their main property is contractility.

  • üê≠Etymology

    The word "muscle" comes from the Latin "musculus," meaning "little mouse," as the shape of some muscles (like the biceps) resembled a mouse.

  • üß¨Origin

    All muscles are derived from the embryonic germ layer called the Mesoderm.

  • üî¥Myoglobin

    A red-colored, oxygen-binding pigment found in muscle fibers, similar to hemoglobin in red blood cells. It stores oxygen within the muscle.

Core Properties of Muscle

  • ‚ö°Excitability (Irritability)

    The ability to receive and respond to a stimulus (e.g., a nerve impulse).

  • üí™Contractility

    The ability to shorten forcibly when adequately stimulated. This is the defining property of muscle tissue.

  • üßòExtensibility

    The ability to be stretched or extended beyond their resting length.

  • üîÑElasticity

    The ability to recoil and resume their original resting length after being stretched.

Skeletal Muscle Layers üßÖ

Connective Tissue Wrappings

üí° Concept: Each muscle is wrapped in layers of connective tissue that support and protect the fibers, and merge to form tendons.

  • üèõÔ∏èEpimysium
    • "Epi" = Upon. The outermost layer.
    • A dense sheath of collagen fibers that surrounds the entire muscle.
    • Separates the muscle from surrounding tissues.
  • üì¶Perimysium
    • "Peri" = Around. The middle layer.
    • Divides the muscle into bundles of fibers called Fascicles.
    • Contains blood vessels and nerves that supply each fascicle.
  • ‚ûñEndomysium
    • "Endo" = Within. The innermost layer.
    • A delicate layer that surrounds each individual muscle fiber (cell).
    • Contains capillaries and nerve endings.

Fascia

  • üßäDeep Fascia

    A tougher layer of dense connective tissue that binds muscles into functional groups (e.g., the quadriceps group).

Tendon & Aponeurosis

At the end of the muscle, all three layers (Epimysium, Perimysium, Endomysium) merge together to form:

  • Tendon: A strong, cord-like structure that attaches muscle to bone.
  • Aponeurosis: A broad, sheet-like tendon that attaches muscle to bone or to other muscles (e.g., in the abdomen).

Fiber & Sarcomere üî¨

The Muscle Fiber (Cell)

  • üñºÔ∏èSarcolemma

    The plasma membrane of the muscle cell. It has a resting membrane potential and can conduct action potentials.

  • üíßSarcoplasm

    The cytoplasm of the muscle cell. Rich in glycogen (for energy) and myoglobin (for oxygen).

  • üß¨Nuclei

    Skeletal muscle fibers are multinucleated (have many nuclei), located just beneath the sarcolemma.

Myofibrils & Myofilaments

  • üßµMyofibrils

    Long, cylindrical structures that run the entire length of the fiber. They are the contractile organelles, composed of myofilaments.

  • ÍµµThick Myofilaments

    Made of the protein Myosin. Has a 'head' that binds to actin and uses ATP.

  • ÏñáThin Myofilaments

    Made of the protein Actin, plus two regulatory proteins: Troponin and Tropomyosin.

The Sarcomere (Functional Unit)

The arrangement of thick (Myosin) and thin (Actin) filaments creates a repeating pattern of bands. One full unit of this pattern is a sarcomere.

Zone Description
Z-Line (Disc) The boundary of one sarcomere. Actin filaments attach here.
A-Band The dark band. Contains the entire length of the Myosin (thick) filament.
I-Band The light band. Contains only Actin (thin) filaments.
H-Zone The lighter region in the middle of the A-Band. Contains only Myosin.
M-Line The very center of the H-Zone. Holds myosin filaments together.

Muscle Contraction ‚ö°

Sliding Filament Theory

üí° Concept: Muscle contraction occurs when the thin filaments (Actin) slide past the thick filaments (Myosin), causing the sarcomere to shorten. The filaments themselves do not change length.

Key Players

  • üß†Neurotransmitter

    Acetylcholine (ACh) is released from a motor neuron at the neuromuscular junction.

  • üîëCalcium Ions (Ca¬≤‚Å∫)

    The "key" that unlocks the contraction. It is released from the Sarcoplasmic Reticulum (SR) in response to the nerve signal.

  • üõ°Ô∏èTroponin & Tropomyosin

    Regulatory proteins on the actin filament. In a resting muscle, Tropomyosin blocks the myosin-binding sites on actin.

  • üîãATP (Energy)

    Powers the "power stroke" of the myosin head and is also required to detach the myosin head from actin (for relaxation).

The Contraction Cycle üîÑ

1. Signal Arrives
ACh binds to receptors on the sarcolemma, starting an action potential.
2. Calcium Release
Action potential travels down T-tubules, triggering Ca¬≤‚Å∫ release from the SR.
3. Binding Sites Exposed
Ca¬≤‚Å∫ binds to Troponin, which pulls Tropomyosin away, exposing the actin binding sites.
4. Cross-Bridge Formation
The energized Myosin head binds to the exposed site on Actin.
5. Power Stroke
The Myosin head pivots, pulling the Actin filament toward the M-line. ADP is released.
6. Detachment
A new ATP molecule binds to the Myosin head, causing it to detach from Actin.
7. Reactivation
The ATP is hydrolyzed (broken down), re-energizing the Myosin head. The cycle repeats as long as Ca¬≤‚Å∫ is present.

Types of Muscle (1/3) üèÉ

Type 1: Skeletal Muscle

  • ü¶¥Attachment

    Primarily attached to the skeleton (bones) via tendons. A few attach to skin (e.g., facial muscles).

  • üß†Control

    Voluntary. Under conscious control from the somatic nervous system. We can choose to move them.

  • üî¨Structure

    Fibers are long, cylindrical, and multinucleated (many nuclei per cell).

  • üìäAppearance

    Striated (striped). This is due to the regular arrangement of sarcomeres (A-bands and I-bands).

  • ‚ö°Contraction

    Contracts rapidly but tires easily. Requires nerve stimulation to contract.

  • üí™Function

    Body movement (locomotion), maintaining posture, stabilizing joints, generating heat (shivering).

  • ü§ùSubtypes (Functional)
    • Flexors: Decrease the angle at a joint (e.g., biceps bending the elbow).
    • Extensors: Increase the angle at a joint (e.g., triceps straightening the elbow).

    Examples: Biceps brachii, Deltoid, Quadriceps femoris, Trapezius.

Types of Muscle (2/3) ‚ù§Ô∏è

Type 2: Smooth Muscle

  • üìçLocation

    Found in the walls of hollow visceral organs (e.g., stomach, intestines, bladder, blood vessels, airways).

  • ü§ñControl

    Involuntary. Under unconscious control from the autonomic nervous system.

  • üî¨Structure

    Cells are spindle-shaped and uninucleate (one central nucleus).

  • üö´Appearance

    Non-striated (smooth). Sarcomeres are not regularly arranged.

  • ‚è≥Contraction

    Contracts slowly and rhythmically. Very resistant to fatigue. Can be stimulated by nerves, hormones, or stretch.

Type 3: Cardiac Muscle

  • üìçLocation

    Found only in the wall of the heart (myocardium).

  • ü§ñControl

    Involuntary. Autonomic nervous system can change the rate, but it contracts on its own (autorhythmic).

  • üî¨Structure

    Cells are branched, uninucleate, and connect at special junctions called Intercalated Discs.

  • üìäAppearance

    Striated (just like skeletal muscle).

  • üîóIntercalated Discs

    These discs contain gap junctions and desmosomes, allowing the heart to contract as a single, coordinated unit.

Muscle Functions üéØ

7 Key Functions of the Muscular System

  • üèÉ1. Movement of the Body

    Skeletal muscles attach to bones and pull on them to produce movement (locomotion), from walking to fine motor skills like writing.

  • üßò2. Maintenance of Posture

    Muscles are in a constant state of partial contraction (muscle tone) that allows us to remain upright (sitting, standing) against gravity.

  • üîó3. Joint Stability

    Muscle tendons cross over joints and the tension from muscle tone helps to stabilize and strengthen them (e.g., the rotator cuff at the shoulder).

  • üî•4. Heat Production (Thermogenesis)

    Muscle contraction generates heat as a byproduct. This is vital for maintaining normal body temperature (e.g., shivering is rapid muscle contraction to generate heat).

  • ‚ù§Ô∏è5. Internal Movement & Support

    Cardiac muscle pumps blood. Smooth muscle moves food through the digestive tract (peristalsis), regulates blood pressure, and pushes urine out of the bladder.

  • üõ°Ô∏è6. Support & Protection

    Muscles, like the abdominal wall, support the weight of visceral organs and protect them from external trauma.

  • üí™7. Maintain Muscle Tone

    A continuous, low-level contraction of muscles that keeps them firm, healthy, and ready to respond to stimuli.

  • 3Ô∏è‚É£Shape

  • 4Ô∏è‚É£Size

    Gluteus Maximus (largest), Peroneus Longus (long).

  • 6Ô∏è‚É£Direction of Fibers

  • 7Ô∏è‚É£Action

The Abdominal Wall

These muscles form a strong, flexible wall that protects internal organs, supports the trunk, and aids in forced breathing, coughing, and childbirth.

  • ||Rectus Abdominis

Clinical Notes ü©∫

Clinical Conditions

  • üòñMyalgia

    General term for muscle pain, weakness, spasms, or inflammation. Can be caused by overuse, injury, or infection.

  • üß¨Muscular Dystrophy

    A group of genetic diseases that cause progressive weakness and loss of muscle mass (muscle wasting).

  • üìâMyasthenia Gravis

    An autoimmune disease where the body attacks and blocks Acetylcholine (ACh) receptors at the neuromuscular junction, leading to severe muscle weakness and poor contraction.

  • üî•Myositis

    Inflammation of skeletal muscle tissue, often caused by infection, injury, or autoimmune disease. Symptoms include pain, swelling, and weakness.
